Get great output

The four habits that decide whether AI saves you time or wastes it: how you brief it, how you manage chats, how you reuse your setup, and which model you pick.

01 · The core skill

Give it a real briefing

In one lineContext is the whole game. Tell it who you are, what you want back, and what to work from.

What separates people who get great results from people who don't is context, not clever wording. Brief the AI like you'd brief a sharp new teammate and it gets a lot better.

A strong request usually has four parts. You don't need all four every time, but when the output is weak, a missing part is almost always why.

  1. Role & situation

    Give the relevant context. "I handle CX for DIGGS and I'm replying to a customer whose crate arrived with a damaged door."

  2. What you want back

    Say it plainly. "Draft a warm, apologetic reply that offers a replacement and explains next steps."

  3. Context & constraints

    Source material, audience, tone, length, things to avoid. "Under 120 words. Warm and premium. Don't promise a delivery date. Here's their message: …"

  4. Format

    Specify the shape. "Give me the email body only, ready to send."

The all-purpose template
I am [role/context].
I need to [goal].
The audience is [audience].
Use this context: [background/source material, reference by name if file attachments].
Constraints: [tone, length, facts to assume, things to avoid].
Return the output as [format].
Tip: You don't have to do this every timeOnce you do a kind of task more than once, a Project or Skill can hold the briefing for you so you never rewrite it.

Where people go wrong

Being too vague
WeakHelp me write an email.
BetterWrite a follow-up to a 3PL contact who missed yesterday's outbound deadline. Professional, firm, under 120 words. Ask for a new ship time and what they're doing to prevent it.
Expecting the first answer to be final

AI works through iteration. Push back: "make it shorter," "less formal," "turn this into a table," "focus more on the risks," "rewrite for a non-technical reader."

Not giving it the source material

Want a summary of a Triple Whale export or a batch of reviews? Paste or upload them. It can only work with what you actually put in front of it.

02 · Critical habit

One chat per task. Start fresh, often.

In one lineNew task, new chat. Long chats get slow, burn your limit faster, and make the outputs worse.

Running one endless chat for everything is the most common mistake I see people make, and it makes everything worse.

Everything in a chat stays in its working memory: every message, every file you drop in. The longer a chat runs, the more crowded that memory gets, and three things happen at once:

  • Responses slow down.
  • You burn through your usage limit faster.
  • Quality drops: the stuff that matters gets buried under everything that came before, or drifts out of view entirely.

So when you switch to a different task, start a new chat. It's free, and it keeps answers fast and sharp. One chat for the launch email, a separate one for the returns SOP, another for the supplier thread.

"But I'll have to re-explain myself"The common fear is re-explaining context every single time. That is exactly what Projects solve, and it's the next section. You keep your instructions and files in one place, and every new chat in that Project already knows them.
03 · The biggest unlock

Stop starting over: Projects, Skills & Artifacts

In one lineDo anything twice? Make a Project or Skill. They hold your context and processes so every new chat already is fully briefed.

These are the features that change how much you get out of Claude. The moment you do the same kind of work twice, stop rebuilding context from scratch.

Project

Where your context lives. Files and instructions every new chat in it already knows.

Skill

How a task gets done. The same steps, the same way, every time.

Artifact

What comes out. A keepable, editable output beside the chat.

Projects — your reusable context

A Project is a persistent workspace for a recurring area of work. Claude remembers the same instructions and reference files across every chat inside it. This is what lets you start fresh chats fearlessly: your setup lives in the Project, not in any single conversation.

Custom instructions

Standing guidance Claude reads at the start of every chat in the Project: your role, goals, tone rules, always and never. About 8,000 characters.

Project knowledge

Files you upload once and Claude references everywhere in the Project. Holds a lot; past that it automatically switches to retrieval so it can still pull from a big library.

Team sharing

Share a Project across the org and set each teammate to Can use or Can edit. How the whole team gets consistent, on-brand output without everyone briefing Claude from scratch.

Live Google Drive files

Add a Google Doc straight from Drive instead of uploading a copy, so Claude always reads the current version. Edit it in Drive and the Project updates itself. Private projects, text only. More in Connectors ↓

Create one:

  1. Go to claude.ai and click Projects in the left sidebar.
  2. Click + New Project, then name it and add a short description.
  3. On our Team plan, choose private or shared with the organization.
  4. Click Set project instructions and add your standing guidance.
  5. Upload reference files to the project knowledge panel on the right.

Skills — reusable procedures

A Skill packages a repeatable procedure so Claude does a type of task the same way every time. Where a Project supplies context (who you are, what to reference), a Skill supplies a method (the steps to follow). Claude has pre-built Skills for making PowerPoint, Excel, Word, and PDF files, and you can build your own.

You don't write a Skill by hand. You have Claude write it for you with the skill creator, a built-in Skill that interviews you and produces the finished Skill itself. Works the same on claude.ai and the desktop app.

  1. In a new chat, ask Claude to create a skill (or pick the skill creator from the Skills list).
  2. Describe the task in plain language: "Draft a reply to a customer review in our voice, following our refund and escalation rules."
  3. Answer its questions. It asks about the steps, what to always or never do, and where it should pause for you.
  4. It hands back a working Skill. Save it under Settings > Features and it's ready to use.
When a Skill earns its keepOnce you've done a task the same way more than a couple of times and you care that it comes out consistent, turn it into a Skill: a weekly reviews-into-themes summary in a set format, a PDP draft that always covers the same sections, a spreadsheet analysis with a consistent deliverable format. True one-offs stay in plain chat.

Artifacts — keepable, editable outputs

An Artifact is a standalone output Claude builds beside the chat instead of burying it in text: a polished document, a table, a chart, a small calculator or tool. Think a reusable shipping-cost calculator, a returns-policy one-pager, a bundle-pricing table.

Ask for one when the output is something you'll keep, refine, or hand off, not just read once. If you would otherwise copy the answer into a doc and keep working on it, an Artifact is the better home.

So which do I use?

You want to…Use
Ask a one-off question or do a quick taskPlain chat
Reuse the same context, files, and standards across many chatsProject
Make Claude follow the same steps the same way every timeSkill
Produce a keepable, editable output (doc, tool, table)Artifact

They combine. A shared Project holds your context, a Skill standardizes a procedure inside it, and the result comes out as an Artifact.

04 · Tools

Choosing a model without getting cut off

In one lineDefault to Sonnet. Save Opus for genuinely hard problems, and touch Fable almost never.

On our Team account you pick the model from a dropdown at the top of the chat. The one thing to understand: capability and cost rise together. The smarter the model, the faster it eats your usage limit.

ModelBest forOn our plan
HaikuFast, simple, high-volume tasksRun it freely.
SonnetEveryday work. The default for almost everything.Run it freely, you won't realistically hit limits.
OpusGenuinely hard reasoning, large or complex analysisUse deliberately. Lean on it for routine work and you can get cut off.
FableThe most capable model that existsExtremely expensive. Expect roughly one query per session before it's spent.
The ruleDefault to Sonnet. Step up to Opus only when something is genuinely hard. Touch Fable almost never, and only for a single high-value question, never for drafting. When in doubt, go one model down: a fresh, well-briefed Sonnet chat beats a bloated Opus one almost every time.

Know its limits

Why a tool this good still gets things confidently wrong, what to never take on faith, and where it genuinely shines versus where it falls down.

05 · Foundation

How it works, and why it's confidently wrong

In one lineIt predicts plausible text, it doesn't look things up. That's why it can sound certain and be wrong.

A language model generates text one small piece at a time, predicting what's likely to come next based on everything it has seen. That one fact explains both its strengths and its biggest flaw. It's great at drafting, summarizing, and restructuring, and it can state something completely false with total confidence, because it's producing plausible text, not looking facts up.

How it goes wrongWhat it looks like
Factual fabricationWrong dates, names, specs, or claims
Fake citationsLinks or sources that don't exist or don't support the claim
Logical inconsistencyThe answer contradicts itself across sections
False confidenceIt sounds certain even when it's wrong
Bottom lineIt's a powerful drafting and reasoning tool that still needs a human check. Sounding sure is not the same as being right.
06 · Foundation

Make it look things up

In one lineRecent, high-stakes, or specific? Verify it. Make it cite a source or search the web rather than trusting its memory.

There are two kinds of facts you should never take on faith.

  1. Anything that may have changed

    Its knowledge is frozen from training, a year or more ago. Prices, product specs, who holds what role, current events, what a tool can do today. For anything time-sensitive, tell it to search the web or give it the current source.

  2. Anything highly specific or high-stakes

    Laws, regulations, tax rules, compliance, exact policy language. Make it cite a source you can actually check, rather than trusting its memory.

The practical rule: trust it for structure and acceleration, not unsupervised facts. If something is recent or highly specific, verify it or feed it the source.

07 · Judgment

What AI is good and bad at

In one lineGreat with words, shaky with facts. Lean on it for language and synthesis, double-check anything with real cost.

It's strongest when the task involves language, structure, synthesis, or pattern recognition.

Great uses

  • Drafting emails, memos, SOPs
  • Summarizing long threads
  • Reviews and NPS into themes
  • Brainstorming angles and bundles
  • Comparing two quotes or directions
  • One story into email, SMS, and ads

Use caution

  • Legal, financial, compliance claims
  • Facts that change frequently
  • Anything needing exact citations
  • Numbers that drive money or inventory
  • Decisions with real cost if wrong

Avoid

  • Letting it take irreversible actions unreviewed
  • Blindly publishing or sending output
  • Treating a first draft as final truth

08 · Tools

Working inside your tools

In one lineLet Claude reach into your tools (browser, Drive, email) for grunt work, with a hand on the wheel. Keep it out of admin, payments, and anything irreversible.

There are two ways to use Claude on the web, and they suit different work.

Switch-to-AI

Open a chat, paste your context, copy the result back into your work. Best for occasional tasks, drafting, and brainstorming.

Work-with-AI

The AI operates inside your browser, docs, or email and sees what you're working on, no endless copy-paste. Best for repetitive, multi-step work.

Claude in Chrome

Claude in Chrome is a browser agent. It can read pages, navigate sites, click, fill forms, manage tabs, and pull data, all from plain instructions. Use it when the task needs you to actually see or touch web pages.

Example: scrape competitor specs
Go through these competitor crate listings open in my tabs, and pull product name, price, dimensions, and weight capacity into a table.
Safe useStart with reading and summarizing. Use trusted sites. Review actions before they run. Never use it for transactions, passwords, or anything irreversible, and don't let it into Shopify admin, payment, or fulfillment systems. Web pages can carry hidden instructions (prompt injection), so keep a hand on the wheel.

Connectors

Connectors plug Claude into the apps we already use, so it can pull from them right inside a chat instead of you copy-pasting. First-party connectors on our Team plan cover Google Drive, Gmail, Google Calendar, GitHub, Microsoft 365, and Slack.

Once connected, Claude can search them and act in them, only when you ask: "find the supplier contract in my Drive and summarize the payment terms," or "pull this week's calendar and draft an agenda." They work in any chat, in a Project or not.

Turning them on is two steps:

  1. An account Owner switches connectors on for the whole org (that's Adam for us). Until that happens, nobody sees them.
  2. Then each person connects their own account under Settings → Connectors, or the + in a chat. Claude only ever sees the account you personally connect.
What it can and can't reachConnecting Google Drive doesn't hand Claude your whole Drive. It reads only what you ask for, only when you ask, and inside a Project, only the files you add. Admins can allow reading while blocking writes and deletes across the org, and Anthropic does not train on connector data. Same rule as everywhere else: let it read and draft freely, keep a human on anything it sends or changes.
09 · Guardrails

Privacy & security

In one lineThe Team account is the safe place to work. Never paste credentials, and when something feels sensitive, ask first.

Our Claude Team account is the approved place to do this work. On commercial Team plans, Anthropic does not train its models on your conversations by default, which is a big reason we chose it over people using random personal AI tools. For the most part, using Claude here for everyday DIGGS work is safe.

Still, use judgment. Before pasting, ask:

  • Is this a password, secret, token, or credential? (Never paste these anywhere.)
  • Is this legally or financially sensitive in a way that needs special handling?
  • Would it be a problem if this ended up outside the company?
Coming soonDetailed data-classification guidance (exactly which customer and business data is cleared for which uses) is coming from Adam. Until then: do the obvious-safe work freely, keep credentials out entirely, and if you're unsure whether something sensitive belongs in a prompt, ask first.

And regardless of the tool: never let AI take an irreversible action (sending, publishing, refunding, moving inventory) without a human reviewing it first.

The core skill isn't memorizing prompts.

It's a handful of habits: brief it well, keep one chat per task, save your setup as a Project, reach for the lightest model that does the job, and verify anything recent or specific. Do that and Claude stops being a novelty and starts being leverage.
